Total Fleet KM
Cumulative GPS-tracked distance across all vehicles for the period. A direct measure of total delivery work done — higher means more ground covered.
Total GPS Hours
Total hours all vehicles were tracked as moving. Compare against crew clocking hours (Crew tab) to see how much time was spent on road vs at the depot.
Avg Utilisation
Average percentage of working days each vehicle was GPS-active, across the fleet. 100% = every vehicle used every working day. Low scores indicate idle or off-road trucks.
Return-to-Base %
Percentage of trip-days where the vehicle's GPS route ended back at the factory depot. Low scores flag overnight away-stops, multi-day cross-border runs, or missing end-of-day GPS data.
Vehicles Tracked
Number of distinct fleet vehicles with GPS data in this reporting period. Use to sanity-check that no trucks are missing from the dataset.

Total KM per Vehicle

Purpose: Ranks each vehicle by total GPS-tracked kilometres over the full period — a direct measure of how much delivery work each truck completed. How to read: Taller bars indicate more active vehicles. A noticeably short bar compared to the fleet average suggests the vehicle was underutilized, off-road for maintenance, or operating on short local routes. Use KM/Hr in the Rankings table to distinguish efficient long-haul trucks from those making many short trips.

Total GPS Hours per Vehicle

Purpose: Shows how many hours each vehicle was tracked as moving over the period. Combined with KM, this reveals route efficiency. How to read: A vehicle with high hours but low KM is travelling slowly — typically due to heavy traffic, many short stops, or dense urban routes. For long-haul delivery trucks a healthy effective speed is 25–40 km/h; divide any vehicle's total KM by its GPS hours to see where it sits.

Fleet Rankings

Purpose: A single table comparing all vehicles across key performance indicators — ideal for management reviews and identifying outliers. How to read: Sorted by total KM (highest first). Utilisation = % of working days the vehicle was GPS-active (green ≥70%, amber ≥40%, red <40%). RTB % = return-to-base rate — % of trip-days the truck ended back at the depot (green ≥80%, amber ≥50%, red <50%). KM/Hr = effective road speed; values below 20 km/h may indicate excessive idling or stop-heavy urban routes. Gold, silver, and bronze badges mark the top 3 performers by distance.

GPS Truck Hours vs Crew Clocking Hours

Purpose: Cross-references GPS vehicle data against biometric clocking data from the HAVEN system to surface mismatches between truck movement and crew time. This is a labour-accountability check. How to read: Each vehicle shows four bars side-by-side: navy = GPS truck hours on road; yellow = driver clocking hours; teal = assistant 1 hours; terra = assistant 2 hours. All clocking hours are filtered to days the truck was GPS-active only, so depot-only days are excluded. A crew bar that is much taller than the GPS bar means crew clocked significant time before/after driving (loading, admin, unloading) — some gap is normal and expected. A very large gap (>30% above GPS hours) warrants a review. GPS bars that are significantly taller than all crew bars may indicate the truck was driven without its regular crew.

Crew Blocking Summary

Purpose: Compact table for at-a-glance comparison of GPS truck time versus individual crew clocking, per vehicle. Use this for payroll cross-checks or to flag anomalies before investigating individual truck cards below. How to read: GPS Hrs = hours the truck was on road (trip days only). Driver / Asst Hrs = biometric clocking hours for each assigned crew member, on the same trip days. Depot Gap = total crew hours minus GPS hours. A positive gap (red badge) is normal — it covers loading, waiting, and unloading time at customers. A large positive gap (>50 hrs) may indicate crew clocking in well before or after truck departure. A negative gap (green badge) means crew clocked fewer hours than the truck moved — possible when a truck was run without its regular crew, or crew data is missing.

Purpose: Gives a bird's-eye view of the entire fleet's activity over time — ideal for spotting patterns, gaps, and high-output days at a glance without reading numbers. How to read: Each row = one vehicle; each column = one working day. Cell colour shows how far the vehicle travelled that day: dark grey = no movement, dark navy ≤100 km, mid navy 101–200 km, bright blue 201–300 km, yellow >300 km (high-intensity). Read across a row to see whether a vehicle works consistently or has large gaps. Read down a column to see how many trucks were active on a specific date — a mostly-grey column may indicate a public holiday or a fleet-wide incident.

Monthly KM per Vehicle

Purpose: Tracks each vehicle's output month by month to reveal trends — growth, seasonal patterns, or a drop-off that needs investigation. How to read: Each colour represents one vehicle (see legend). A vehicle with a lower bar in a later month is not necessarily performing worse — check the Monthly Breakdown table below for active days. Fewer working days in a month will naturally produce lower KM totals. To compare months fairly, divide each vehicle's KM by its active days in that month.

Monthly Breakdown

Purpose: The detailed complement to the chart above — provides raw numbers for KM, GPS hours, and active days per vehicle per month, enabling proper month-on-month normalization. How to read: Always check the Days column first. If a vehicle had 22 active days in March but only 8 in April, its April KM will naturally be much lower — that is not a performance issue. To compare efficiency across months, calculate KM ÷ Days for each row. The Total KM column (right) is the full-period sum and matches the Overview charts.

Top Destinations (day end-points)

Purpose: Shows where each truck's GPS route ended at close of day. Identifies the most common delivery endpoints and flags vehicles that did not return to base. How to read: The Olympic Paints Factory (Anchorville) should dominate — it confirms that vehicles returned to the depot. Rows highlighted in green are factory returns. Non-factory destinations represent overnight stops, cross-border deliveries, or routes that finished at a customer site. A high count of non-factory destinations for a specific vehicle is worth investigating.

Top Origins (day start-points)

Purpose: Shows where each tracked route began. Confirms that vehicles are dispatching from the factory and identifies trucks that started their day away from the depot. How to read: Green-highlighted rows are factory dispatches (normal). Non-factory origins mean the vehicle was parked overnight at a remote location from the previous day's run. Cross-referencing origins against destinations on a date-by-date basis lets you reconstruct multi-day routes and confirm driver reconciliation.
